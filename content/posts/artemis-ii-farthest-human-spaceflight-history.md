Four humans just flew past the Moon and went farther from Earth than any person in history. Not just farther than any living person — farther than _any human who has ever existed_. The previous record stood for fifty-six years, set by a crew that was essentially screaming "abort, abort, abort" for most of their mission.

And what were we doing in the meantime? Launching satellites. Building space stations. Letting billionaire man-children crash their own cars into desert sand while posing for Instagram. But going _past_ the Moon? Nah, that was too ambitious. That was for some other generation.

## The Numbers

On April 6, 2026, the Artemis II crew hit 252,756 miles from Earth. Let me say that again: _two hundred fifty-two thousand, seven hundred fifty-six miles_. The previous record was 248,655 miles, set by Apollo 13 in 1970. Yes, the same Apollo 13. The one with the exploding oxygen tank. The one where everyone on Earth held their breath waiting to see if they'd make it back alive.

That's the thing that gets me. The furthest any human has ever been from this planet was achieved by a mission that was, by any reasonable definition, a catastrophic failure. The lunar landing was canceled. Two astronauts nearly froze to death. Jim Lovell and Jack Swigert spent most of that mission trying not to die. And yet, somehow, that disaster got them further from Earth than anything we'd do for the next half-century.

It turns out that when you strap a human to a bomb and point it at the Moon, you go far. When you actually _plan_ to go far? That takes decades of meetings.

## The Flyby Itself

If you missed it: Artemis II launched on April 1st, 2026 — because apparently NASA has a sick sense of humor and launched on April Fool's Day. The mission was a ten-day lunar flyby, which means these four idiots (lovingly: Reid Wiseman, Christina Koch, Victor Glover, and Jeremy Hansen) floated around the Moon at thousands of miles per hour and came right back. No landing. No stepping out. Just "hey, let's go past the Moon and see what happens."

What happened was they broke a record that had been gathering dust for over five decades. They also reportedly saw a solar eclipse from lunar orbit, which sounds like something out of a sci-fi movie and not something you'd expect from a mission that's technically just "a practice run" before Artemis III actually lands people.

The whole thing makes you realize how much we've collectively slumped. We put twelve people on the Moon between 1969 and 1972. Then we stopped. We spent fifty years with our thumbs up our asses, and now we're celebrating flying _past_ the Moon like it's some incredible achievement. Which, to be fair, it is — but can you imagine if we'd just... kept going? If the post-Apollo space program hadn't gotten gutted?

## The Real Achievement

Here's the thing nobody's talking about: this mission almost didn't happen. Artemis II was delayed for years. YEARS. Every few months there was another problem, another cost overrun, another "we need more time to review the data." SLS was mocked as the "Senate Launch System" — a rocket so expensive that economists actually cried doing the cost analysis. And through all of that, the program limped forward one crisis at a time.

Now four people are on their way back to Earth, having gone farther than anyone in human history. The toilet works. Outlook still doesn't, but that's beside the point. They did it. They actually did it.

I'm not going to sit here and pretend that Artemis II is some kind of vindication of the entire space program, because there's legitimate criticism in how we got here and where we're going. But watching humans fly past the Moon — watching them go _farther_ than any human ever — there's something deeply, fundamentally human about that. It's the thing we do. We climb things. We fly past things. We point ourselves at the horizon and say "bet."

Now the question is what the hell we do next. Because "almost landing on the Moon" is only phase one. Phase three is supposedly boots on the ground, and god knows when that happens. But right now, today, four people are closer to another world than any person has ever been, and they're coming home.

That's not nothing. That's actually kind of incredible. Even if Outlook is broken.
