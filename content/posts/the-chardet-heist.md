Five days. That's how long it took Dan Blanchard to rewrite chardet — a library with 130 million monthly downloads, a dependency lurking in the dependency tree of half the Python ecosystem, the quiet workhorse that saves developers from the hell of guessing character encodings. Five days with Claude, and suddenly the entire codebase is "new." New license. New structure. New everything. The LGPL is gone, replaced with a permissive MIT license, and the entire open source world is staring at a legal gray area so massive you could drive a truck through it.

This is the chardet heist, and it's the most important thing to happen to software licensing since someone first typed "LICENSE" into a text file.

Here's what happened. Blanchard has maintained chardet for years, mostly alone, mostly unpaid, watching it get 130 million downloads a month while the world mostly forgot it existed. He wanted it in the Python standard library. There's just one problem: the LGPL. The standard library folks don't want copyleft licenses cluttering up their pristine collection of "batteries included." So Blanchard needed to change the license. But that's not how LGPL works — you can't just decide one day that the code you inherited is now MIT. The license travels with the code. Derivatives must remain under the same terms.

So instead of asking nicely or negotiating with the FSF, Blanchard did what any good 2026 developer would do: he prompted his way to a solution. He fed Claude the old chardet code — not the whole thing at once, presumably, but enough for the model to learn what character encoding detection looks like — and had it rewrite the entire library from scratch. The resulting code, according to JPlag (a plagiarism detection tool), has less than 1.3% similarity to any prior version. No single file structurally resembles its predecessor. It's, technically, a clean room implementation. It's also, technically, a complete workaround of every principle that copyleft was designed to protect.

And now we have a problem. A massive, industry-shaking problem that nobody has the answers to.

The core question is this: does "I didn't copy the code, I just used an AI that learned from the code" constitute a derivative work? Mark Pilgrim — or someone claiming to be him, which is its own delicious layer of drama — opened an issue titled "No right to relicense this project" that got over 1,400 positive reactions in days. The issue argues that Blanchard's claim of a clean room implementation is irrelevant because "they had ample exposure to the originally licensed code." Adding a "fancy code generator" into the mix doesn't grant any additional rights. The GPL and LGPL exist specifically to prevent this exact game — taking code, modifying it, and then stripping away the freedoms that came with it. The argument is that AI hasn't changed the fundamental ethics, even if it's changed the technical execution.

But here's where it gets genuinely complicated. The code genuinely is different. JPlag shows less than 1.3% similarity. The detection speed is apparently 48x faster. This isn't a case of renaming variables and calling it a new project. The AI genuinely produced new code that solves the same problem in a different way, trained on a model that learned from the old code. It's like hiring a ghostwriter who's read all your previous books and asking them to write something similar in a different genre. Is it plagiarism? Legally? Probably not. Ethically? That's where everyone's teeth start showing.

Armin Ronacher — yes, the Flask guy, the one who's been in this ecosystem long enough to remember what open source looked like before it was a marketing term — wrote a blog post that basically said "good, finally." His argument is chilling in its honesty: copyleft depends on copyright and friction to enforce itself, but "because it's fundamentally in the open, with or without tests, you can trivially rewrite it these days." When the cost of generating code drops to essentially zero and you can reimplement anything from documentation and test suites alone, what does licensing even mean? What is a derivative work when an AI can dream one up from a specification?

And Bruce Perens — the guy who literally wrote the Open Source Definition — went full alarmist. He's out there saying the "entire economics of software development are dead, gone, over, kaput." He described the experience of prompting an AI to build an SRE platform from promotional materials and documentation alone as feeling like "abracadabra." He says proprietary software companies that depend on "holding close something that can be cloned as easily as I did" won't survive. He's not wrong. He's just early.

The Free Software Foundation, naturally, is not pleased. Zoë Kooyman told The Register that there's nothing "clean" about an LLM that's ingested the code it's being asked to reimplement. Undermining copyleft is "highly antisocial," regardless of the method. This is worth underlining: the FSF, the organization that built the entire philosophical framework for free software, is saying that AI rewrites of copyleft code are a direct attack on user freedom. They're not mincing words. They're not treating this as a nuanced technical question. They're treating it as an existential threat.

And here's the part that should keep every open source maintainer up at night: this works. It demonstrably works. Five days. No team of lawyers. No elaborate workaround. Just a prompt and patience. If Blanchard can do it with chardet, anyone can do it with anything. SQLite? Sure. FFmpeg? Absolutely. Some random abandoned library on npm with two stars and a README that's mostly apology? That's the dream scenario. The entire "I built this, I license it however I want" framework assumes that the work of building is the hard part. AI has made building trivially easy. What remains is the legal fiction — the idea that there's something sacred about the act of typing code into a file yourself versus telling a machine to do it.

This is why this matters beyond the chardet drama. This is the opening move in a game where the rules haven't been written yet. Every copyleft project on the planet now has to ask itself a question it never had to ask before: what happens when someone feeds your entire codebase to an AI, rewrites it in a week, and releases it under a permissive license? You can sue, probably. You might even win, eventually, after years of litigation that costs more than your project ever received in donations. Or you can watch as someone else monetizes the work you poured your spare time into for a decade, except now it's faster and has a more startup-friendly license.

The really ugly part is the philosophical question nobody wants to answer honestly. Copyleft was always a bet — the bet that forcing derivatives to remain free would create a world where more software stayed free. It was a social contract: you get to use my code, but you have to pass it forward. The enforcement mechanism was always the difficulty of rewriting code. If it's trivial to rewrite, the contract is void. The "share-alike" clause only matters if sharing is the cheaper option. AI has made "not sharing" the cheaper option. The entire ethical framework of copyleft depends on the assumption that copying is harder than writing. That assumption is dead.

And Dan Blanchard? He's not some villain rubbing his hands in a lair. He's a maintainer who wanted his library in the standard library, who saw a path around the obstacle, and who took it. He did it in five days because he was tired of waiting. He's even listed Claude as a contributor, which is either a brilliant legal move or a fascinating admission that the code isn't entirely his. The whole thing reads like someone who just wanted to get stuff done and accidentally broke the foundational assumptions of an entire licensing philosophy.

This is the future we're living in. Not the future where AI writes your unit tests or suggests variable names — the future where AI makes the entire concept of software licensing look like a quaint artifact from a time when humans actually wrote code. The chardet heist isn't a bug. It's a feature. It's the first loud signal that the rules have changed, that the old protections don't cover the new reality, and that everyone's going to have to figure out what comes next while the lawyers catch up and the FSF writes increasingly frantic position papers.

Five days. 1.3% similarity. 130 million downloads a month. Nothing was carried forward. Everything changed.
